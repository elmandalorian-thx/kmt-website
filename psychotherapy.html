Quick Guide

Which Therapy Is Right for You?

Use this guide to find the therapeutic approach that matches your needs.

If You're Experiencing... We Recommend
Personal challenges like anxiety, depression, stress, or life transitions Individual Therapy
Relationship difficulties, communication issues, or trust concerns with your partner Couples Therapy
Trauma, PTSD, disturbing memories, or flashbacks EMDR Therapy
Family conflict, parenting challenges, or communication breakdown at home Family Therapy
Teen struggling with anxiety, identity, peer pressure, or academic stress Teen Counseling
Parent-child relationship repair after divorce or separation Reunification Therapy

Your first session is primarily about getting to know you and understanding your concerns. We'll discuss what brought you to therapy, your goals, and what you hope to achieve. This is also a chance for you to ask questions and see if you feel comfortable with your therapist. There's no pressure to share more than you're ready to.
The length of therapy varies depending on your concerns and goals. Some people find relief in a few sessions, while others benefit from longer-term support. We'll work together to determine what's right for you and regularly check in on your progress. You're always in control of how long you continue therapy.
EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing) is an evidence-based therapy for processing trauma and distressing memories. During EMDR, you'll focus on a traumatic memory while following bilateral stimulation (usually eye movements). This helps your brain process the memory naturally, reducing its emotional intensity. EMDR has been extensively researched and is highly effective for PTSD, anxiety, and other trauma-related conditions.
Yes, psychotherapy is covered by most extended health benefit plans in Ontario. Coverage varies by provider and plan type. We provide detailed receipts for insurance submission. Couples therapy can help if you're experiencing communication difficulties, frequent conflicts, trust issues, intimacy concerns, or major life transitions. It's not just for relationships in crisis—many couples seek therapy to strengthen their connection and prevent future problems. Both partners should be willing to participate and work on the relationship.
